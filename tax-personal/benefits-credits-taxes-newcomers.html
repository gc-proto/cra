Benefits, credits, and taxes for newcomers

What you need to know the first year you are a resident of Canada.

Are you a newcomer to Canada?

You are considered a newcomer to Canada for the first year you are a resident of Canada for income tax purposes.

You become a resident of Canada for income tax purposes when you establish significant residential ties in Canada. You usually establish these ties on the date you arrive in Canada.

Newcomers to Canada who have established residential ties may be:

  • Refugees
  • People who have applied for or received permanent resident status
  • People who have received "approval-in-principle" from Immigration, Refugees and Citizenship Canada to stay in Canada

Get a social insurance number (SIN)

Service Canada: Apply for a SIN

[As a newcomer to Canada, you will need a social insurance number (SIN). The SIN is a nine-digit identification number that is unique, personal, and confidential.]

[To work in Canada and receive benefits and services from the government, you need a social insurance number, also called a SIN.]

A SIN is a 9 digit identification number that is unique to you. You are the only person who is supposed to use it, and you are responsible for keeping it safe. If your SIN gets into the wrong hands, it could lead to fraud or even identity theft.]

[You will need to give your SIN to your employer when you start a job and to your bank when you open an account. They are responsible for protecting your personal information. They use your SIN to send information about your income to the CRA.]

[The CRA uses your SIN to identify you for income tax and benefit purposes. You have to give your SIN to anyone who prepares tax information slips (such as a T4 slip) for you.]

[If Service Canada gave you a temporary SIN (starting with the number 9) or if the CRA gave you a temporary tax number (TTN) or an individual tax number (ITN), and you decide to become a permanent resident of Canada, you have to apply for a SIN with Service Canada. Once this new SIN has been assigned to you, do not use any other SIN, TTN, or ITN that was previously assigned to you.]

[If you cannot get a SIN, you will be provided with a TTN or ITN]: Tax numbers (SIN, ITN, or TTN)

What money can you get?

Canada has benefits and credits to help financially support you. To start getting payments, you must

  1. Get a social insurance number (SIN) from Service Canada
  2. Apply for each benefit and credit you are eligible for

As soon as you get your social insurance number, apply for benefits. You do not need to have your first return filed before getting benefits and credits.

To get these benefits and credits, you or your spouse or common-law partner must be one of the following:

  • a permanent resident, even with a temporary address
  • a protected person

18 month requirement for temporary residents

If you are a temporary resident, you must live in Canada for 18 months in a row and have a valid permit on your 19th month of living in Canada before you can apply for the Canada child benefit and any related provincial and territorial programs.

After you apply for benefits and credits

Only send in an application for benefits and credits one time.

To continue receiving the benefit and credit payments you are entitled to, every year you must:

  • file your income tax and benefit return on time, even if you have no income in the year
  • have your spouse or common-law partner, if you have one, file a return even if they have no income
  • keep your personal information updated with the CRA

  • keep documents that show proof of your situation, in case we ask for them as part of the validation process

Do you have to file a tax return?

[One sentence about what a tax return is.]

You have to file a tax return if you have to pay tax or if you want to claim a refund, even if you only lived in Canada for part of the year.

You do not need to file a tax return before you can begin receiving the Canada Child Benefit or the Goods and Services Tax Credit. However, you do need to file a tax return to continue getting these credits after your first year in Canada, even if you had no income.

Be aware of tax scams

[Preamble placeholder]

  • [Common scams - need to research what authoritative content to point to & which scams to outline]

Scams could be by phone, email, mail or instant messaging.

The Canada Revenue Agency (CRA) and Immigration, Refugees and Citizenship Canada (IRCC) will never:

  • contact you over the phone to collect fees or fines
  • be aggressive or threaten to arrest or deport you
  • threaten to harm you or a member of your family, or damage your home or property
  • ask you to pay fees using prepaid credit cards, Western Union, gift cards, or any other similar services

  • For in-depth information, refer to: scam prevention and the CRA
