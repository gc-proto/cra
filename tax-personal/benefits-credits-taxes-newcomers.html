Benefits, credits, and taxes for newcomers

What you need to know the first year you are a resident of Canada.

[Who a newcomer to Canada is]

You are considered a newcomer to Canada for the first year you are a resident of Canada for income tax purposes.

You become a resident of Canada for income tax purposes when you establish significant residential ties in Canada. You usually establish these ties on the date you arrive in Canada.

Newcomers to Canada who have established residential ties may be:

  • refugees
  • permanent residents
  • people who have received "approval-in-principle" from Immigration, Refugees and Citizenship Canada to stay in Canada

Temporary residents, international students, may be considered residents (deemed residents) of Canada for income tax purposes in some cases.

Read more about how to determine your residency status and what residential ties are.]

Returning to Canada after being a non-resident

If you were a resident of Canada in a previous year and you are now a non-resident, you will be considered a resident of Canada for income tax purposes when you move back to Canada and re-establish your residential ties.

Learn about Canadian taxes

Many of the benefits people enjoy in Canada are made possible through taxes. If you are an employee, your employer usually deducts taxes and pays them to the CRA on your behalf.

Each year, people complete an Income Tax and Benefit Return (a tax return) to find out if they owe tax or if they will get a tax refund.

Start by getting a social insurance number (SIN)

A SIN is a 9-digit number that is personal, confidential, and unique to you.

You need a SIN to:

  • receive benefits and credits
  • work in Canada
  • open a bank account

Go to Service Canada to apply for a SIN.

What to do if you can't get a SIN

If Service Canada is unable to give you a permanent or temporary SIN, CRA may give you a temporary tax number (TTN) you may use to get benefits and credits, file taxes, and sign up for CRA's online services.

If you are a non-resident, CRA may give you an individual tax number (ITN).

How to get a TTN or ITN

Complete your benefit or credit application [or tax return - to be verified] without an account number. Attach a letter explaining why you do not have a SIN or can't get one.

To get a number faster, you may also include certified copies of documents that prove your identity, such as:

  • passport
  • driver's licence
  • birth certificate or proof of birth
  • if your spouse has a SIN and is sponsoring you, an immigration document showing that your spouse is sponsoring you
  • a letter from Citizenship and Immigration Canada stating they are reviewing your file

Whether you get a TTN or an ITN depends on your residency status when we review your request.

What money you could get

Canada has benefits and credits to help financially support you. To start getting payments, you must:

  1. Get a social insurance number (SIN) from Service Canada
  2. Apply for each benefit and credit you are eligible for

As soon as you get your social insurance number, apply for benefits. You do not need to do your first tax return before you can get benefits and credits.

Eligibility for benefits and credits

To get these benefits and credits, you or your spouse or common-law partner must be either:

  • a permanent resident, even with a temporary address
  • a protected person

If you are a temporary resident, you must live in Canada for 18 months in a row and have a valid permit on your 19th month of living in Canada before you can apply for the Canada child benefit and any related provincial and territorial programs.

Which benefits and credits you can apply for

Canada child benefit
If you have at least one child under 18 years old, apply for a tax-free monthly payment to help with the cost of raising your family.
Apply for the Canada child benefit
Goods and services tax/harmonized sales tax (GST/HST) credit
If you are at least 19 years old and have a low or modest income, apply for a tax-free quarterly payment to help offset the GST or HST you pay on things you buy.
Apply for the GST/HST credit
Provincial and territorial benefits and credits
The provinces and territories also have several benefits and credits that you may be eligible for.
You don't need to apply separately for related provincial and territorial programs. We will determine your eligibility when you apply for the Canada child benefit or with the information provided in your tax return.

Keep getting your benefits and credits

Only send in an application for benefits and credits one time. [Are there separate applications though?]

To continue receiving the benefit and credit payments you are entitled to, every year you must:

  • file your income tax and benefit return on time, even if you have no income in the year
  • have your spouse or common-law partner, if you have one, file a return even if they have no income
  • keep your personal information updated with the CRA

  • keep documents that show proof of your situation, in case we ask for them as part of the validation process

[When you need to do your taxes]

Even if you only lived in Canada for part of the year, you have to do your taxes if either:

  • you have to pay tax
  • you want to claim a refund

You do not need to do your taxes before you can begin receiving the Canada Child Benefit or the Goods and Services Tax Credit. However, you do need to file a tax return to continue getting these credits after your first year in Canada, even if you had no income.

For in-depth information, refer to: Do you have to file a return?

Doing your taxes for the first time in Canada

You can fill in your tax return and send it to the CRA online or by paper. You do not need to hire someone to do your taxes for you. Read more about how to fill in and send us your tax return.

Be aware of tax scams

[Intro sentence explaining that there are scams to be aware of and possibly listing the "welcome to Canada" scam as an example.]

Scams may contact you by phone, email, mail or instant messaging.

We will never:

  • contact you over the phone to collect fees or fines
  • be aggressive or threaten to arrest or deport you
  • threaten to harm you or a member of your family, or damage your home or property
  • ask you to pay fees using prepaid credit cards, Western Union, gift cards, or any other similar services

For in-depth information, refer to: scam prevention and the CRA

Free tax help from the CRA

If you have a modest income and a simple tax situation you may be able to use a free tax clinic to get help completing your tax return.

If you are self-employed or own a small business and need help understanding your tax obligations, you can get free in-person tax help from the CRA’s liaison officer service.

We hold free learning events for different communities, including newcomers. Join us at one of our upcoming tax learning events.
