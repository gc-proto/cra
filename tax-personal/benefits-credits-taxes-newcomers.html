Benefits, credits, and taxes for newcomers

What you need to know the first year you are a resident of Canada.

Are you a newcomer to Canada?

You are considered a newcomer to Canada for the first year that you are a new resident of Canada for income tax purposes.

You become a resident of Canada for income tax purposes when you establish significant residential ties in Canada. You usually establish these ties on the date you arrive in Canada.

Newcomers to Canada who have established residential ties with Canada may be:

  • protected persons (including refugees) within the meaning of the Immigration and Refugee Protection Act
  • people who have applied for or received permanent resident status from Immigration, Refugees and Citizenship Canada
  • people who have received "approval-in-principle" from Immigration, Refugees and Citizenship Canada to stay in Canada

If you were a resident of Canada in an earlier year, and you are now a non-resident, you will be considered a resident of Canada for income tax purposes when you move back to Canada and re-establish your residential ties.

What money [financial support?] can you get?

Canada has benefits and credits to help financially support you. To start getting payments, you must

  1. Get a social insurance number (SIN) from Service Canada
  2. Apply for each benefit and credit you are eligible for

As soon as you get your social insurance number, apply for benefits.

To get these benefits and credits, you or your spouse or common-law partner must be one of the following:

  • a permanent resident, even with a temporary address
  • a protected person

18 month requirement for temporary residents

If you are a temporary resident, you must live in Canada for 18 months in a row and have a valid permit on your 19th month of living in Canada before you can apply for the Canada child benefit and any related provincial and territorial programs.

After you apply for benefits and credits

You don’t have to send in an application for the benefits and credits every year. But every year you must:

  • file your income tax and benefit return on time every year, even if you have no income in the year. If you have a spouse or common-law partner, they also have to file a return every year.
  • keep your personal information updated with the CRA

  • keep your supporting documents in case the CRA asks for them as part of the validation process

Should you file a tax return?

[One sentence about what a tax return is.]

As a newcomer, you do not need to file a tax return before you can begin receiving the Canada Child Benefit or the Goods and Services Tax Credit. However, you do need to file a tax return to continue getting these credits after your first year in Canada.

You also have to file a tax return if you have to pay tax or if you want to claim a refund, even if you only lived in Canada for part of the year.

For in-depth information, refer to: Do you have to file a return?.
